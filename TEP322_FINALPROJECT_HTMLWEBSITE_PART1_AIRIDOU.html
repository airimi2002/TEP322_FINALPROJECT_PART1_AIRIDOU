New York and Toronto, Not so Different After All

By Aaron "Airi" Dou  ·  April 8, 2026

Zohran Mamdani, the newly elected New York City mayor won not because of his wealth, or his elitism, but he offered the opposite. His most compelling claims are: freezing the rent, lowering public commute prices, and lessening the burden for childcare costs. Basic human rights. Not a dream nor a utopia, but a simple vision for the city- a city where people can live without breaking their bank accounts. People called him "radical", where his claims are normalized in Europe [1].

Have our standards lowered that much?

What does this have to do with Toronto? Sure, Toronto is not New York City, but you would see that we are far more similar than different. When was the last time you went to Loblaws and felt shocked that grocery prices rose again? When was the last time you paid your rent and watched your paycheck disappear? When was the last time you took the TTC worried that you'd either accidentally inhale secondhand crack or harassed by power-tripping fare inspectors? Not so different from New York City after all, are we. Zohran argues that New York City belongs to the working people of the city; So let me ask one last question:

When was the last time you felt like this city belonged to you?

My answer is never; Or at least not after the sad passing of Rob Ford, an anti-elitest mayor for the people [2], may he rest in peace. The people of this city deserve more, we deserve a city that does not rob us of a place to live, we deserve a city that does not rob us of a way to commute, we deserve a city that allows for the next generation to prosper, not suffer. Toronto should learn from Zohran.

No Place Left to Live

By Aaron "Airi" Dou  ·  April 8, 2026

I remember last summer where my roommates and I searched for a new place to live. Clicks after clicks, hope slowly turned to despair- not because there aren't places to live, just not ones affordable for two full-time employees, much less adding a student (myself) to the mix. The average rent in Toronto costs over $2,900 a month [3], and considering the average income for Torontonians to be $62,000 a year [4], that is over half your salary spent just to have a roof over your head.

Over half the average Torontonian salary spent on rent.

In 2018, the province removed rent control in hopes to motivate new rental constructions [3]. One would think that would lower rent prices, after all, more houses should equate to lower housing prices; The opposite happened, prices skyrocketed upwards of 35% in the following years [5].

Uncontrolled rent allows landlords to unreasonably jack prices upwards. According to CCPA [6]:

"Between 2014 and 2023, Ontario's annual rent increase guidelines officially allowed landlords to increase residential rents by 16.5 per cent. Yet over that period, average rents actually went up by 54.5 per cent, more than three times the guideline rate."

The freedom to live where you wish. To live near your family, to live near your friends, to live near your job or school, it all hinges on the mercy of your landlord. Although not impossible to rent a place, it means by doing so, you are sacrificing over half your hard earned paycheck for a basic necessity. Toronto should take notes from Zohran.

No Place Left to Go

By Aaron "Airi" Dou  ·  April 8, 2026

I remember the last time I took the TTC Streetcar: I was late to school. Frequent delays, closures, and increasing costs leads the people to lose trust in Toronto's main form of transit [7]. The dependence on fare revenue is a major flaw, fewer riders directly means less income.

How do you expect to fix a broken system by telling the people to use it more?

The mindset of people thinking: "If they can neglect us, then we can neglect them", and placing fare inspectors does not help the issue. Seeing how people have been mistreated and harassed by them [8], it only paints more of a negative picture of the current transit system.

It isn't much better if you drive a car; Constant traffic on the 401 highway, hiking gas prices (more expensive than California!), and the cost of insurance makes it miserable for commuters.

Did you know that Brampton has the highest automotive insurance rate in the entirety of North America? [9]

Regardless if you drive a car, take the streetcar, or ride the subway; Regardless if you're a student commuting from work, a nurse ending a 12-hour shift dreading the ttc ride back home, or a parent stuck in traffic after dropping their kids off at school; We can all agree that Toronto's commute is one of the worst things about the city. Toronto should be more like Zohran's NYC.

No Place for the New Generation

By Aaron "Airi" Dou  ·  April 8, 2026

I have had this conversation many times from older friends and family:

"Would you ever consider having a child?"
"Haha, no."
"Oh, why not?"
"It's too expensive."

"It's too expensive."

Other than subsidized daycare programs, the average cost for infant daycare in Toronto costs over $1,700 a month [10], almost, if not more than the cost of rent. How do you expect a parent to work and take care of their infant child? The simple answer is: you can't. Having a child in Toronto is not a question, it's impossible.

Toronto should aim to create a city where the future generation can be a possibility, not a hopeless dream.

For those who decided to try anyways, the waitlist for childcare is already at maximum capacity. Ever since the province of Ontario joined the "national $10-a-day program", the waitlist for childcare skyrocketed to unprecedented degrees [11]. Aspiring parents in Toronto are constantly advised to register their child years before they are born, for a chance at a spot for childcare after spending years in a queue.

Although this program is a step towards a positive future, it is a broken-promise fueled by staffing shortages, underfunded budgeting, and a program unable to meet the demand it itself created. For such a big city that Toronto is, parenthood should not be a waiting list, and the next generation shouldn't be a hopeless dream. Toronto should take a page from Zohran.

Toronto Can't be THAT Bad Right?

By Aaron "Airi" Dou  ·  April 8, 2026

So what if rent increases? Just build more houses, just remove zoning restrictions, just let competition bring each other's prices down. The argument goes much deeper than these obvious, although incomplete, sentiments. Houses were built, restrictions were removed, and competition did occur.

Prices are still higher than ever.

Increasing supply is merely a bandaid on the systemic issue of governmental policies. It doesn't help building more houses if it costs more to sell than to build it [12]; That's backwards logic. The government needs to start treating housing as a public necessity, the government should provide easier opportunities for the people to have a roof over their head. I'm sure the people who got forcefully kicked out of their tents in Trinity Bellwoods would agree [13].

So implement new policies, make it easier for the people.

That is also a naive understanding of the bigger issue: Time. There is no more time for people to wait, their landlord demands a paycheck within the next month, their work still requires them to commute tomorrow morning, and their children's mouths need to be fed tonight.

Increased rent, underfunded transit, and a complete utter lack of universal childcare are not a result of "the market" or "the economy". The market and economy is a result of poor political choices, aimed to benefit the wealthy elites, not the people.

The people are left with no more money to live, and even less patience to wait for it to change.

Toronto needs to learn from Zohran, now.
